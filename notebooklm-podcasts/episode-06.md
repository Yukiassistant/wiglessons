## Lesson 46: Anime Hair Forms

Objective: Translate drawings into buildable shapes.

Core concept: Drawn hair can be reduced into major chunks: bangs, side pieces, crown volume, spikes, ponytails, buns, and loose ends.

Practice prompt: Name the three biggest chunks in one character hairstyle.

Check question: Which chunk matters most for recognition?

Ready when: You can reduce a style to major forms.

Key terms:
- silhouette: The outer shape of the hairstyle, usually the first thing viewers recognize.
- character read: How quickly the hairstyle is recognizable as the intended character.

## Lesson 47: Chunk Hierarchy

Objective: Rank pieces by visual importance.

Core concept: Primary chunks control the silhouette. Secondary chunks support the style, and detail chunks can be simplified if they do not change the character read.

Practice prompt: Rank three hair chunks as primary, secondary, or detail.

Check question: Which piece could be simplified?

Ready when: You can prioritize chunks.

Key terms:
- character read: How quickly the hairstyle is recognizable as the intended character.

## Lesson 49: Soft Spike

Objective: Identify spikes that can stay flexible.

Core concept: Short spikes may only need cutting, heat direction, texture, and product. Longer spikes usually need hidden support.

Practice prompt: Choose whether one spike is short enough to stay soft.

Check question: What would make it need support?

Ready when: You can classify a short spike.

Key terms:
- spike: A pointed or lifted hair shape that may need texture, product, heat, or support.
- setting: Shaping synthetic fiber with heat, steam, tension, clips, or rollers until it cools.

## Lesson 50: Supported Spike

Objective: Know when hair needs help.

Core concept: Longer or heavier spikes need a support plan before surface styling. More spray is not the plan.

Practice prompt: Pick wire or foam for a pretend long spike and say why.

Check question: Which support is lighter or safer here?

Ready when: You can choose a support type.

Key terms:
- wire support: Wire used to hold shape, spread load, or connect a piece to anchors.
- foam core: A lightweight foam support used inside oversized spikes, buns, curls, or shapes.

## Lesson 51: Shake Test

Objective: Test movement early.

Core concept: A shake test reveals whether a spike, ponytail, or attachment is actually secure before final finishing.

Practice prompt: Write what you would watch during a gentle shake test.

Check question: What failure would be acceptable in a mockup?

Ready when: You can define a movement test.

Key terms:
- shake test: A movement test that checks whether a style or attachment survives motion.
- anchor: The point or structure that carries pull, weight, clips, straps, or supports.

## Lesson 52: Wefts Add More Than Hair

Objective: Understand weft jobs.

Core concept: Wefts can add density, color, coverage, repair material, and direction control. They also add weight.

Practice prompt: Name one reason to add a weft besides making hair fuller.

Check question: What problem could added weight create?

Ready when: You can name two weft jobs.

Key terms:
- weft: A sewn strip of wig hair that can be added, removed, moved, or harvested.
- density: How much hair is present in an area; higher density looks fuller but adds weight.

## Lesson 53: Donor Wig Concept

Objective: Use extra hair deliberately.

Core concept: A donor wig provides matching or contrasting fiber for wefts, repairs, hairlines, or covering supports.

Practice prompt: Name one part of a build that could use donor wig fiber.

Check question: Does the fiber need to match exactly?

Ready when: You can name one donor-wig use.

Key terms:
- donor wig: A second wig used for extra fiber, wefts, color sections, or repairs.
- coverage: How well hair, fiber sheets, lace, or panels hide supports, gaps, and construction.

## Lesson 54: Weft Direction

Objective: Think before sewing.

Core concept: The direction a weft points affects how hair falls and what it covers. Placement is a design decision.

Practice prompt: Draw one arrow showing how an added weft should fall.

Check question: What would look wrong if it pointed the other way?

Ready when: You can plan weft direction.

Key terms:
- hair direction: The direction fibers travel so the style, seams, and coverage look intentional.
- coverage: How well hair, fiber sheets, lace, or panels hide supports, gaps, and construction.
