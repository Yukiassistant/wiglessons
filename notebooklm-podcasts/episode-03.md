# Source Lessons

## Lesson 19: Steam As Moist Heat

Objective: Know what steam is for.

Core concept: Steam can relax or reshape synthetic fiber with less direct contact than a hot iron, but steam still heats the fiber.

Practice prompt: Write one job steam might do: relax, smooth, reshape, or reset.

Check question: Which steam job changes the fiber shape?

Ready when: You can name one steam use and one caution.

Key terms:
- setting: Shaping synthetic fiber with heat, steam, tension, clips, or rollers until it cools.
- fiber memory: Synthetic fiber's tendency to keep the shape it cooled or was stored in.

## Lesson 20: Heat Log

Objective: Make heat repeatable.

Core concept: A heat log records tool, temperature, time, and result so a successful strand test can be repeated on the visible wig.

Practice prompt: Create a four-field heat log template.

Check question: Which field records the exact temperature?

Ready when: You have a reusable heat-test note.

Key terms:
- strand test: A hidden test on a small fiber section before using heat, dye, or product broadly.
- heat-resistant: Able to tolerate controlled heat better than standard synthetic fiber, but still meltable.

## Lesson 21: Detangle Ends First

Objective: Avoid making knots worse.

Core concept: Detangle from the ends upward. Starting at the roots pushes knots tighter and increases friction frizz.

Practice prompt: Write the order: ends, middle, roots.

Check question: What causes frizz besides tangles?

Ready when: You know the detangling direction.

Key terms:
- detangling: Removing knots gently, usually from ends upward, to avoid tightening tangles.
- friction frizz: Frizz caused by rubbing against clothes, hands, collars, bags, or storage surfaces.

## Lesson 22: Storage Shape

Objective: Store for the silhouette.

Core concept: Storage should protect the shape you built. A loose wig and a spiked wig should not be packed the same way.

Practice prompt: Name one storage risk for a long wig and one for a styled wig.

Check question: What would get crushed first?

Ready when: You can choose storage based on shape.

Key terms:
- silhouette: The outer shape of the hairstyle, usually the first thing viewers recognize.
- coverage: How well hair, fiber sheets, lace, or panels hide supports, gaps, and construction.

## Lesson 24: Cleaning Caution

Objective: Know when not to wash aggressively.

Core concept: Lace, product, glued pieces, and structured shapes can be damaged by rough cleaning. Match cleaning pressure to the most delicate part of the wig.

Practice prompt: Name one part of a styled wig you would clean gently.

Check question: What could loosen or frizz?

Ready when: You can name one cleaning risk.

Key terms:
- edge: A visible boundary such as hairline, lace, sideburn, nape, shell rim, or seam.
- coverage: How well hair, fiber sheets, lace, or panels hide supports, gaps, and construction.

## Lesson 26: Cutting Is Permanent

Objective: Start conservative.

Core concept: Synthetic wig cutting is unforgiving. Leave extra length, test the fall, then refine.

Practice prompt: Choose a pretend bang length, then write a safer longer first cut.

Check question: Where would overcutting hurt most?

Ready when: You know the first cut should leave room.

Key terms:
- rough cut: The first conservative cut that removes length while leaving room to refine.
- sectioning: Dividing hair into controlled areas before cutting, heating, teasing, or styling.

## Lesson 27: Section Before Cutting

Objective: Control what you cut.

Core concept: Sectioning isolates the hair being cut from display layers, support layers, and hair that still needs length for blending.

Practice prompt: Describe one section you would clip away before trimming bangs.

Check question: What hair should not be cut yet?

Ready when: You can name one protected section.

Key terms:
- sectioning: Dividing hair into controlled areas before cutting, heating, teasing, or styling.
- surface layer: The visible hair layer that hides support work and gives the final finish.
